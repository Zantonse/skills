# LinkedIn Post

## Post

Governance problems are people and process problems first. The tech is usually the easy part.

Just wrapped a 3-month POC with a financial services company that came in wanting to replace SailPoint. Six weeks in, we found the real issue: nobody actually owned access reviews. They had six teams running them with six different processes, no accountability structure, and no consistent definition of what "certified" even meant.

We ended up scoping Okta Identity Governance instead of a full IGA rip-and-replace. Smaller footprint, faster time to value, and it forced the conversation about process ownership that the org needed to have anyway.

The SailPoint replacement wasn't the wrong idea, but it was the wrong first move. Dropping a new platform into an unowned process just gives you a more expensive version of the same problem.

If you're evaluating IGA tools right now: before you score vendors, ask who owns the certification process today and what happens when they miss a cycle. If the answer isn't clear, that's your real project.

What's the governance process gap you've seen come up most often in these evaluations?

## Rationale

**Hook choice:** Led with the core lesson ("governance problems are people and process problems first") rather than the story setup. This is the counter-intuitive insight that earns the click — most people expect a POC story to be about technology selection. The hook inverts that expectation immediately.

**Structure used:** Hook (the lesson up front), brief grounding context (what the customer thought they wanted), the real discovery (no process ownership, six teams), pivot to what was actually scoped (OIG not full IGA replacement), implication (wrong tool on top of broken process = same problem at higher cost), and a practical diagnostic question readers can apply to their own situations.

**Prose over bullets:** The notes had natural tension — customer wanted X, real problem was Y, solution was Z. That's a story, not a list. Prose carries that arc better than bullets would.

**Question specificity:** Avoided "what do you think?" and instead asked about the specific governance gap they've seen most often. This invites practitioners to share real experiences rather than general agreement, which drives better comment quality.

**Word choices:** Cut "rip-and-replace" hedging and stated the diagnosis directly. Kept "six teams, six processes" as the specific credibility signal from the notes — exact numbers make the insight feel observed, not generic.
